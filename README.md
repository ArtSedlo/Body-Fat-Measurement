**Executive Summary**

Accurate assessment of whole-body fat mass is essential for managing obesity, malnutrition, and metabolic disorders, which are increasing globally. While conventional anthropometric methods like BMI, waist circumference, and skinfold thickness are accessible and low-cost, they lack precision in distinguishing between fat and lean tissue or in capturing fat distribution. Bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA) is non-invasive, affordable, and scalable—though it has limitations in detecting regional or ectopic fat. In contrast, radiological techniques (e.g., dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry, computed tomography and magnetic resonance imaging) offer high accuracy of fat mass assessment for a high price, resulting in lower accessibility of these techniques. This project aims to systematically compare anthropometric, bioelectrical, and radiological methods of fat mass assessment in patients with and without obesity. The comparison will evaluate accuracy against a reference standard, cost-effectiveness, and technological/regulatory limitations to guide clinical and research practice. The study will validate screening tools for obesity and reveal measurement biases across different populations.

Body fat distribution and composition are critical determinants of health outcomes, disease risk, and physical performance. Accurate assessment of whole-body fat mass (WBFM) is essential for managing obesity, malnutrition, and metabolic disorders — conditions with dramatically rising global prevalence in recent decades (1).  In cases with involuntary loss of weight due to severe diseases or ageing, accurate body composition assessment enables cachexia monitoring through tracking fat-free mass loss (2). The existing tools and protocols for examining the whole body composition possess the following limitations. First, most of them are costly and inaccessible to the wide population. Second, few of them reflect adipose and lean tissue distribution. Herein, we provide a short review of the strengths and limitations of major methods for assessing body composition.  

**Conventional anthropometric methods** are widely used due to their simplicity, low cost, and applicability in clinical and epidemiological settings (3). However, these techniques for assessing body composition suffer from significant limitations that compromise their diagnostic precision. Examples are body mass index (BMI), waist circumference (WC), skinfold thickness, and waist-to-hip ratio (WHR).

 _BMI measurements_ are practical for population-level screening, but they fail to differentiate between fat mass, lean mass, and bone density, nor do they capture fat distribution (4). Consequently, individuals with identical BMIs may exhibit vastly different health profiles due to disparities in visceral adiposity, muscle mass, or ectopic fat deposition.  
_WC and WHR_ provide only a crude approximation of central obesity, neglecting variations in subcutaneous vs. visceral fat or muscle composition (5).  
_Skinfold measurements_ are highly operator-dependent, and they cannot assess deep or ectopic fat compartments (e.g., hepatic or intramuscular fat) (6). These shortcomings are particularly consequential in pre-obesity or atypical phenotypes (e.g., sarcopenic obesity or metabolically healthy obesity), where early fat distribution changes may precede metabolic dysfunction. 

**Radiological imaging techniques** offer superior accuracy for body composition assessment compared to conventional anthropometric methods (7). These modalities enable precise quantification of fat mass, lean mass, bone mineral density, and adipose tissue distribution, including differentiation between visceral and subcutaneous fat depots.  
_Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA)_ is a highly reproducible and pretty safe tool suitable for research and clinical applications (8). 
_Computed tomography (CT)_ and _magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)_ are gold standards for evaluating visceral adiposity and ectopic fat deposition (e.g., hepatic or intramuscular fat). However, their utility is constrained by significant limitations: high costs, restricted availability in resource-limited settings, and, for CT, ionizing radiation exposure risks (9).   
Despite drawbacks, radiological methods can advance obesity research, validating surrogate anthropometric measures, and informing targeted interventions based on individualized fat distribution patterns.  
**Bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA)** is a widely used, non-invasive method for assessing body composition, particularly estimates of fat mass, fat-free mass, and total body water (10). The technique operates by measuring the resistance and reactance of body tissues to a low-level electrical current, which varies according to the tissue’s water and electrolyte content. Its key advantages are affordability, portability, and safety, making it suitable for both clinical and large-scale epidemiological settings.  
Physicians should measure BIA in standard conditions and calculate body composition with equations created and tested on the specific population of people. Still, the accuracy of assessing body composition with BIA can be compromised by factors such as hydration status, recent physical activity, food intake, and ambient temperature. Furthermore, standard BIA devices offer limited insight into regional fat distribution and cannot distinguish between subcutaneous and visceral adipose tissue or detect ectopic fat accumulation in organs such as the liver or skeletal muscle (11).  
**Reference techniques for research**. To compare the accuracy of different tests, we will use the reference standards: the 4-compartment (4C) model and air displacement plethysmography (ADP). These are advanced techniques for measuring body composition, particularly fat mass. Due to its complexity, the 4C model is used primarily in research. It requires multiple devices (e.g., DXA + deuterium dilution). ADP serves as a practical alternative to the 4C model. 
Aim and objectives. This project aims to systematically compare anthropometric, bioelectrical, and radiological methods of fat mass assessment in patients with and without obesity. The comparison will evaluate accuracy against a reference standard, cost-effectiveness, and technological/regulatory limitations to guide clinical and research practice.
